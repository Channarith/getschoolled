LESSON: Emergency Critical Thinking and Mental Readiness
LANGUAGE: en

SLIDE 1 | Why Emergency Thinking Is a Trainable Skill
Research in aviation, medicine, and military operations consistently shows
that performance under extreme pressure is not fixed — it is trainable.
The brain's threat-response system (amygdala) can be conditioned through
graduated exposure so that calm, deliberate reasoning remains accessible
even when adrenaline spikes. This is the foundation of emergency critical
thinking training.
NARRATION: Today we explore how elite professionals in aviation, medicine,
and crisis management train their brains to stay sharp when everything goes wrong.
This is not about removing fear — it is about channelling it.

SLIDE 2 | The OODA Loop: Think at the Speed of Events
The OODA loop — Observe, Orient, Decide, Act — was developed by fighter
pilot and military strategist John Boyd. It describes how any thinking
agent processes reality and responds. The key insight: faster OODA loops
win. An expert does not think through each step consciously — they
compress the loop through pattern recognition built in training.
- Observe: gather raw data from the environment
- Orient: filter through experience, culture, mental models
- Decide: select a course of action
- Act: execute and immediately start a new observation cycle
NARRATION: The OODA loop is why a chess grandmaster can play 40 opponents
simultaneously. Pattern recognition replaces deliberation. We train this
same compression in emergency scenarios.

SLIDE 3 | Situational Awareness: Levels 1, 2, and 3
Endsley's model defines situational awareness across three levels:
- Level 1 (Perception): detecting the elements in the environment
  (instruments, people, sounds, changes)
- Level 2 (Comprehension): understanding what those elements mean
  (this EGT rise + OAT = probable carburetor ice)
- Level 3 (Projection): predicting how the situation will evolve
  (if I do nothing, I have 4 minutes before the engine quits)
Loss of situational awareness is the root cause of most accidents. The
fix is structured scan patterns and pre-brief of threat cues.
NARRATION: Level 3 — projection — is what separates a reactive response
from a proactive one. We will practice building all three levels.

SLIDE 4 | The DECIDE Model for Emergency Decisions
Used widely in aviation and emergency services, DECIDE provides a
checklist to prevent rushed, incomplete decisions:
- Detect: identify that a problem exists
- Estimate: assess urgency and consequences of inaction
- Choose: generate and select from multiple options (never only one)
- Identify: list resources, people, and time needed
- Do: execute with commitment
- Evaluate: monitor the outcome and be ready to switch plans
The model slows down the impulse to act on the first idea and forces
the decision-maker to consider at least two alternatives.
NARRATION: The most dangerous phrase in an emergency is "there's only one option."
DECIDE forces you to prove it. Eighty percent of the time, you will find a better one.

SLIDE 5 | Pre-Mortem Analysis: Failing Before You Begin
Gary Klein's pre-mortem technique asks: imagine it is six months from now
and this plan has failed catastrophically. What went wrong? Working
backwards from failure is cognitively easier and more productive than
trying to predict failure from the starting point. It surfaces:
- Hidden assumptions that have not been tested
- Single points of failure (no backup)
- Over-optimistic timelines
- Human factors risks (fatigue, distraction, overconfidence)
NARRATION: A pre-mortem is not pessimism — it is responsible planning.
Aviation and surgery use this technique because the cost of discovering
a flaw in training is zero; the cost during an actual emergency can be catastrophic.

SLIDE 6 | Threat and Error Management (TEM)
TEM is a framework from aviation Crew Resource Management. It organises
every factor that could cause an error into three categories:
1. Threats — hazards outside our direct control (weather, equipment faults,
   workload spikes, system failures)
2. Errors — our own mistakes in response (misidentify, forget, rush)
3. Undesired states — the dangerous situation that results when threats
   and errors interact
Good TEM practice means identifying threats BEFORE they trigger errors,
and building explicit countermeasures into the plan.
NARRATION: TEM is the reason that a commercial airline with two pilots
and multiple checklists rarely has accidents. It is not redundancy for
its own sake — it is systematic threat identification baked into operations.

SLIDE 7 | Mental Rehearsal: The Flight Sim in Your Head
Mental rehearsal (or mental simulation) is the controlled practice of a
scenario in the mind's eye with the same emotional engagement as the real
event. Studies with Olympic athletes, surgeons, and fighter pilots show
measurable performance improvement from mental rehearsal alone. Effective
rehearsal includes:
- Specificity: rehearse the exact procedure step by step
- Sensory anchors: include sounds, feelings, visual cues
- Decision gates: rehearse choosing, not just doing
- Adversity: include one thing going wrong and recovering
NARRATION: When an airline captain says "I've done this approach a thousand
times," half of those times may have been in a simulator or in their head.
We will guide you through your first structured mental rehearsal today.

SLIDE 8 | Stress Inoculation: Graduated Exposure
Stress inoculation training (SIT) involves three phases:
1. Conceptual: understand the stress response and why it affects performance
2. Skills acquisition: learn the procedures in a low-stress environment
3. Application: practice under progressively increasing stress
The goal is not immunity to stress — that would remove the performance
boost that moderate arousal provides (Yerkes-Dodson law). The goal is
the ability to function in the optimal performance zone even when the
situation feels catastrophic.
NARRATION: This is the science behind why astronauts train in water tanks,
why trauma surgeons practice on cadavers, and why hostage negotiators
do role-play before real incidents. Graduated exposure calibrates the
stress response so it works for you, not against you.

SLIDE 9 | Critical Thinking Under Time Pressure
When time is short, cognitive shortcuts (heuristics) take over from
deliberate reasoning. Many of these shortcuts are useful — pattern
recognition is fast and often correct. But they also introduce
predictable errors:
- Confirmation bias: seeking evidence that confirms the first hypothesis
- Anchoring: fixating on the first number or option
- Availability bias: overweighting vivid, recent, or dramatic events
- Fixation error: locking onto one explanation and ignoring new data
Training countermeasures: maintain a short "competing hypothesis" list,
build in explicit "am I wrong?" check-ins, and use checklists to bypass
individual memory.
NARRATION: Top-gun pilots are taught to ask "what else could this be?"
as a reflex. We can train that same reflex — and this module will start you on that path.

SLIDE 10 | Emergency Landing Decision Framework
Using the engine-failure emergency as a worked example, we walk through
the decision framework that all pilots must internalize:
1. Aviate first: establish best-glide speed (65 kts for C172)
2. Navigate: select the best landing site within glide range
3. Communicate: Mayday call on 121.5 / squawk 7700 (never first)
The critical insight: altitude is time. A pilot who delays best-glide
by 10 seconds loses 150–300 feet of altitude they can never recover.
The decision framework must be so well rehearsed that it executes
reflexively, freeing cognitive capacity for the field-selection problem.
NARRATION: The most survivable forced landing is not the one with the
best field — it is the one where the pilot established best glide
within 3 seconds. We will practice this decision until it becomes automatic.

SLIDE 11 | Emotional Regulation in Emergencies
High-performance emergency response requires emotional regulation, not
emotional suppression. Physiological arousal (adrenaline, cortisol) is
unavoidable — and partly beneficial. The target is the "performance zone":
aroused enough to act quickly, calm enough to reason clearly.
Techniques that work under training conditions:
- Tactical breathing (box breathing: 4-4-4-4)
- Positive self-talk anchors ("I am trained for this")
- Pre-brief of the most likely stressor (the unexpected becomes expected)
- Pair awareness (monitoring your partner's state and your own)
NARRATION: Research on combat soldiers, firefighters, and ER physicians
consistently shows that the best performers are not the ones who feel
no fear — they are the ones who have learned to act despite it.
