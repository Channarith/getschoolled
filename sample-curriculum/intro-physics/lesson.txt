Introduction to Mechanics

Welcome to a first look at how objects move and exchange energy. We will connect
everyday motion — a pendulum, a ball in flight — to equations you can predict
with before you see the demo.

Energy and Conservation

Kinetic energy is the energy of motion: KE = (1/2) m v^2.
Potential energy stored by height is PE = m g h.
In a closed system with no friction, total mechanical energy stays constant:
  E = KE + PE = constant.

Example: Pendulum

A simple pendulum swings back and forth. At the highest point it momentarily stops:
all energy is potential. At the bottom it moves fastest: energy is mostly kinetic.
If you know the release height, you can predict the speed at the bottom without
measuring it — that is the power of conservation.

Demo: Predict Before You Look

Before releasing the pendulum from height h = 0.50 m, predict the speed at the
bottom. Use g = 9.8 m/s^2 and m = 0.20 kg. The math says v ≈ 3.1 m/s — watch
whether the demo matches your guess.

Exercise

A 0.15 kg ball drops 1.2 m with negligible air drag. Compute its speed just
before it hits the ground using energy conservation. Check units: m/s.

Summary

Mechanics is about prediction: write the energy balance, solve for the unknown,
then test with a measurement. Come back and redo one calculation on paper today.
