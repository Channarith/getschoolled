LESSON: AI and Machine Learning Fellowship
LANGUAGE: en
AUDIENCE: corporate
TRACK: AI
LEVEL: Level 6 Apprenticeship
ROLE: Machine Learning Engineer
DELIVERY: 16 month delivery, plus 3 month assessment
FIT: Data science teams.
SUMMARY: Turn raw data into production machine learning, and weave ML responsibly into how your organisation works.

SLIDE 1 | What This Course Is About
This fellowship is for practitioners who will build and ship machine learning, not
just talk about it. It covers the full lifecycle: framing a problem, preparing
data, training and evaluating models, deploying them, and keeping them healthy in
production. It assumes comfort with Python and basic statistics, and it treats
machine learning as an engineering discipline where reliability, measurement, and
responsibility matter as much as model accuracy.
NARRATION: This fellowship takes you through the full ML lifecycle, from framing a problem to running it in production.

SLIDE 2 | What Machine Learning Really Is
Machine learning is the practice of building systems that learn patterns from
data to make predictions or decisions, instead of following hand-written rules.
The shift in mindset is important: you do not program the answer, you provide
examples and an objective, and the algorithm finds a function that fits. This is
powerful when rules are too complex to write by hand, but it also means the model
is only ever as good and as fair as the data and objective you give it.
NARRATION: In ML you supply examples and an objective; the algorithm learns a function instead of being told the rules.

SLIDE 3 | Supervised, Unsupervised, and Reinforcement Learning
There are three broad families. Supervised learning learns from labeled examples
to predict a target, such as classifying email as spam or predicting a price.
Unsupervised learning finds structure in unlabeled data, such as clustering
customers into segments. Reinforcement learning learns by trial and error through
rewards, as in game-playing or robotics. Most business value today comes from
supervised learning because labeled outcomes are common.
NARRATION: Supervised learning predicts from labels, unsupervised finds structure, reinforcement learns from rewards.

SLIDE 4 | Framing the Problem
The most expensive mistakes happen before any model is trained. Frame the problem
as a precise prediction tied to a decision and a measurable business outcome:
what exactly are we predicting, for whom, when, and what action follows. Decide
how you will know it worked. A technically excellent model that predicts the
wrong thing, or one no one acts on, is wasted work. Good framing aligns the model
with value.
NARRATION: Frame ML as a precise prediction tied to a decision and a measurable outcome before training anything.

SLIDE 5 | Data Preparation and Feature Engineering
Real-world data is messy, and practitioners spend most of their time here.
Cleaning handles missing values, outliers, and inconsistent formats. Feature
engineering transforms raw fields into inputs a model can learn from, such as
turning a timestamp into day-of-week or encoding categories as numbers. The
features you craft often matter more than the choice of algorithm, because a
model can only learn from the signal you expose to it.
NARRATION: Most ML work is data prep and feature engineering; good features matter more than the algorithm choice.

SLIDE 6 | Training, Validation, and Test Splits
To estimate how a model will perform on new data, you must hold some data back.
Train on one portion, tune choices on a separate validation set, and report final
performance on a test set the model has never seen. If you make decisions using
the test set, it stops being an honest estimate. This discipline is what
separates a real performance claim from wishful thinking.
NARRATION: Split data into train, validation, and test sets; keep the test set untouched until the final estimate.

SLIDE 7 | Overfitting and Underfitting
A model underfits when it is too simple to capture the pattern, doing poorly even
on training data. It overfits when it memorizes the training data, including its
noise, and then fails on new data. The goal is generalization: good performance
on data it has not seen. The gap between training and validation performance is
your main warning sign of overfitting.
NARRATION: Underfitting is too simple, overfitting is memorizing noise; aim for generalization to unseen data.

SLIDE 8 | The Bias-Variance Tradeoff
Generalization error has two competing sources. Bias is error from overly simple
assumptions that miss the real pattern; variance is error from being too
sensitive to the particular training sample. Simpler models tend to have high
bias and low variance, complex models the reverse. Good modeling finds the sweet
spot, often using techniques like regularization to control complexity.
NARRATION: Bias is error from oversimplifying, variance from oversensitivity; tune complexity to balance them.

SLIDE 9 | Common Algorithms and When to Use Them
Start simple. Linear and logistic regression are fast, interpretable baselines.
Decision trees capture nonlinear rules but overfit alone, which is why ensembles
like random forests and gradient boosting, which combine many trees, are
workhorses for tabular data. Neural networks excel on unstructured data like
images, audio, and text. A strong simple baseline first tells you whether
complexity is even worth it.
NARRATION: Begin with simple baselines; ensembles dominate tabular data and neural nets shine on unstructured data.

SLIDE 10 | Evaluating Models Honestly
Accuracy alone is misleading, especially with imbalanced data: a model that
always predicts the majority class can look accurate while being useless. Use
precision and recall to understand the kinds of mistakes, the F1 score to balance
them, and metrics like ROC AUC for ranking quality. Choose the metric that
matches the cost of errors in your problem, because a false positive and a false
negative rarely cost the same.
NARRATION: Go beyond accuracy; precision, recall, and F1 reveal error types, and the right metric matches error cost.

SLIDE 11 | Deep Learning and Neural Networks
Neural networks stack layers of simple units that transform inputs into
increasingly abstract representations, learning features automatically rather
than by hand. Deep learning powers modern image, speech, and language systems.
The cost is that networks need large data and compute, are harder to interpret,
and are easier to fool, so they are a tool for the right problem, not a default.
NARRATION: Neural networks learn features automatically and power modern AI, but need lots of data and are hard to interpret.

SLIDE 12 | Large Language Models and Foundation Models
Foundation models, including large language models, are trained on vast data and
adapted to many tasks. Instead of training from scratch, teams now often fine-tune
or prompt a pretrained model, and ground it in their own data through retrieval.
This shifts much ML work from building models to adapting, evaluating, and safely
integrating them. Their fluency can mask errors, so evaluation remains essential.
NARRATION: Foundation models are pretrained and adapted by fine-tuning, prompting, or retrieval rather than built from scratch.

SLIDE 13 | From Notebook to Production: MLOps
A model in a notebook delivers no value until it runs reliably where decisions
happen. MLOps applies software engineering to ML: version your data, code, and
models; automate training and deployment pipelines; and serve predictions through
monitored, testable services. Reproducibility matters: you should be able to
rebuild any model from recorded data and code. Most ML projects fail in this gap,
not in the math.
NARRATION: MLOps brings versioning, pipelines, and reliable serving so models actually run where decisions are made.

SLIDE 14 | Monitoring and Model Drift
Models decay because the world changes. Data drift is when incoming data differs
from training data; concept drift is when the relationship you learned no longer
holds. A model that was accurate at launch can quietly degrade. Monitor input
distributions and live performance, set alerts, and plan to retrain. A deployed
model is a living system that needs ongoing care, not a finished artifact.
NARRATION: Models drift as the world changes; monitor inputs and performance, and retrain before they quietly degrade.

SLIDE 15 | Responsible AI: Fairness and Bias
Because models learn from historical data, they can reproduce and amplify human
bias, performing worse for some groups or encoding past discrimination. Test
performance across subgroups, not just overall, and decide what fairness means
for your context. High-stakes decisions about people, like lending or hiring,
demand extra scrutiny, human oversight, and the ability to challenge an outcome.
NARRATION: Models can amplify bias from historical data; test across subgroups and keep humans in high-stakes decisions.

SLIDE 16 | Interpretability and Trust
Stakeholders rarely trust a black box, and regulations increasingly require
explanations. Use interpretable models where you can, and explanation techniques
that show which features drove a prediction where you cannot. Being able to say
why a model made a decision supports debugging, builds trust, and is often a
legal necessity. Interpretability is part of the engineering, not an afterthought.
NARRATION: Explain why a model predicts what it does; interpretability builds trust and is often legally required.

SLIDE 17 | Privacy, Security, and Governance
ML systems concentrate sensitive data and create new risks. Follow data
protection rules, minimize and protect the data you use, and document datasets and
models so their limits are known. Models can be attacked through poisoned data or
crafted inputs, and can leak information about their training data. Treat data
governance and security as core requirements, agreed with the business, not as
optional extras.
NARRATION: Protect and document data, guard against attacks and leakage, and treat governance as a core requirement.

SLIDE 18 | Course Summary and Next Steps
You now have the full lifecycle: frame the problem around a decision, prepare data
and craft features, train and evaluate honestly, deploy with MLOps, monitor for
drift, and embed fairness, interpretability, and governance throughout. The
strongest ML engineers pair technical depth with judgment about value and risk.
Pick a real problem, build the simplest baseline that could work, and iterate
toward production.
NARRATION: Master the lifecycle and pair technical depth with judgment; start simple and iterate toward production.
