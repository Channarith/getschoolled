LESSON: Intro to Fractions
LANGUAGE: en

SLIDE 1 | What is a Fraction?
A fraction represents a part of a whole. It is written as a numerator over a
denominator, for example one half is written 1/2.
NARRATION: A fraction shows part of a whole, like one half of a pizza.

SLIDE 2 | Numerator and Denominator
The denominator is the bottom number and tells how many equal parts the whole is
divided into. The numerator is the top number and tells how many parts we have.
NARRATION: The bottom number counts the total parts; the top number counts what we have.

SLIDE 3 | Equivalent Fractions
Two fractions are equivalent if they represent the same value, like 1/2 and 2/4.
You can find them by multiplying or dividing numerator and denominator by the
same number.
NARRATION: One half equals two quarters; equivalent fractions name the same amount.

SLIDE 4 | Simplifying Fractions
A fraction is in lowest terms when the numerator and denominator share no common
factor other than one. Divide both by their greatest common factor to simplify,
so 4/8 becomes 1/2.
NARRATION: Divide top and bottom by their greatest common factor to write it simply.

SLIDE 5 | Comparing Fractions
To compare fractions, give them the same denominator and compare the numerators.
For example, 2/3 and 3/5 become 10/15 and 9/15, so 2/3 is larger.
NARRATION: Make the denominators match, then the bigger numerator is the bigger fraction.

SLIDE 6 | Adding Fractions
To add fractions with the same denominator, add the numerators and keep the
denominator. For different denominators, first rewrite them over a common
denominator, then add.
NARRATION: With the same bottom number, just add the top numbers.

SLIDE 7 | Subtracting Fractions
Subtraction works the same way as addition: use a common denominator, then
subtract the numerators. For example, 3/4 minus 1/4 equals 2/4, which simplifies
to 1/2.
NARRATION: Find a common denominator, then subtract the top numbers.

SLIDE 8 | Multiplying Fractions
To multiply fractions, multiply the numerators together and the denominators
together. You do not need a common denominator. For example, 2/3 times 4/5
equals 8/15.
NARRATION: Multiply the tops, multiply the bottoms - no common denominator needed.

SLIDE 9 | Dividing Fractions
To divide by a fraction, multiply by its reciprocal - flip the second fraction
upside down. For example, 1/2 divided by 3/4 equals 1/2 times 4/3, which is 4/6
or 2/3.
NARRATION: Dividing by a fraction is the same as multiplying by its flipped form.

SLIDE 10 | Mixed Numbers and Improper Fractions
A mixed number combines a whole number and a fraction, like 1 1/2. An improper
fraction has a numerator larger than its denominator, like 3/2. You can convert
between the two.
NARRATION: One and a half can also be written as the improper fraction three halves.

SLIDE 11 | Fractions, Decimals, and Percents
A fraction can be written as a decimal by dividing the numerator by the
denominator, and as a percent by multiplying that decimal by one hundred. So 1/2
equals 0.5 equals 50 percent.
NARRATION: One half is the same as the decimal 0.5 and as 50 percent.

FACT: The denominator of a fraction can never be zero.
FACT: A fraction equals one whole when the numerator equals the denominator.
FACT: Equivalent fractions have the same value but different numbers.
FACT: A fraction is in lowest terms when numerator and denominator share no common factor but one.
FACT: To divide by a fraction, multiply by its reciprocal.
FACT: To multiply fractions, multiply the numerators and multiply the denominators.
FACT: One half equals the decimal 0.5 and 50 percent.
