LESSON: Data Fellowship
LANGUAGE: en
AUDIENCE: corporate
TRACK: Data
LEVEL: Level 4 Apprenticeship
ROLE: Data Analyst
DELIVERY: 13 month delivery
FIT: Aspiring and new data analysts.
SUMMARY: Empower your team with programming, data modelling, and analysis skills to unlock the full potential of your data.

SLIDE 1 | What This Course Is About
This fellowship builds the core skills of a working data analyst: getting data,
cleaning and modelling it, analysing it with code and statistics, and
communicating findings that change decisions. It assumes no prior programming and
takes you to the point where you can independently answer real business questions
with data. The emphasis is on practical analysis you can apply to your
organisation's own data.
NARRATION: This fellowship builds practical data analyst skills, from getting and cleaning data to analysing and communicating it.

SLIDE 2 | The Analyst's Workflow
Analysis follows a repeatable loop: understand the question, gather the data, clean
and shape it, explore and analyse, then communicate the result and recommend an
action. Most beginners rush to charts; strong analysts spend their time framing the
question and preparing the data, because that is where accuracy and insight are won
or lost. Learn the whole loop, not just the visible end.
NARRATION: The analyst loop is question, gather, clean, analyse, communicate; framing and prep matter most.

SLIDE 3 | Asking Analytical Questions
A useful analysis starts with a specific, decision-linked question, such as which
customer segment has the highest churn and why, not a vague "how are we doing." A
sharp question defines what data you need and what a good answer looks like.
Translating a fuzzy business request into a precise, answerable question is one of
the most valuable skills an analyst has.
NARRATION: Turn vague requests into specific, decision-linked questions; that defines the data and the answer.

SLIDE 4 | SQL: Getting the Data
Most business data lives in relational databases, and SQL is the language for
asking it questions. With SELECT, WHERE, GROUP BY, and JOIN you can filter, summarise,
and combine tables to assemble exactly the dataset your question needs. SQL is the
analyst's most-used tool because it lets you pull and shape data at the source
rather than wrestling exports in a spreadsheet.
NARRATION: SQL filters, aggregates, and joins tables so you can pull exactly the data your question needs.

SLIDE 5 | Data Modelling and Structure
How data is organised shapes what you can do with it. Understand tables, keys, and
relationships, and the idea of tidy data where each row is an observation and each
column a variable. Well-structured data makes analysis straightforward; messy,
inconsistent structure makes every step harder. Part of an analyst's job is
reshaping data into a clean, analysable model.
NARRATION: Understand tables, keys, and tidy data; clean structure makes every later step easier.

SLIDE 6 | Cleaning and Preparing Data
Real data is messy: missing values, duplicates, inconsistent formats, and errors.
Cleaning, deciding how to handle gaps, removing duplicates, standardising
categories and dates, is unglamorous but decisive, because analysis built on dirty
data is confidently wrong. Document the choices you make while cleaning so your work
is transparent and reproducible.
NARRATION: Clean data by handling missing values, duplicates, and inconsistencies; document your choices.

SLIDE 7 | Programming for Analysis
Code, typically Python with libraries like pandas, lets you analyse data at a scale
and repeatability spreadsheets cannot match. You load data, transform it, compute
summaries, and produce charts in a script you can rerun and audit. Programming turns
a one-off manual analysis into a reliable, repeatable process, which matters as
questions recur and datasets grow.
NARRATION: Programming, e.g. Python with pandas, makes analysis scalable, repeatable, and auditable.

SLIDE 8 | Descriptive Statistics and Distributions
Summarise data honestly: use the mean and median to describe typical values,
knowing the median resists extremes, and always look at the spread and the shape of
the distribution. A single number hides detail, and an average can describe nobody.
Understanding variation and distribution is what separates a real finding from a
misleading headline.
NARRATION: Summarise with mean, median, and spread, and read the distribution; averages can hide or mislead.

SLIDE 9 | Exploratory Data Analysis
Before drawing conclusions, explore: chart distributions, compare groups, look for
trends and outliers, and check relationships between variables. Exploration builds
intuition, surfaces data-quality problems, and generates hypotheses worth testing.
It is detective work, and it almost always changes your initial assumptions about
what the data says.
NARRATION: Explore data with charts and comparisons to build intuition, find issues, and form hypotheses.

SLIDE 10 | Correlation, Causation, and Significance
Two variables moving together does not prove one causes the other; a hidden factor
may drive both. And a difference between groups may be random noise rather than a
real effect. Analysts must be careful not to over-claim: distinguish correlation
from causation, and understand that apparent patterns need enough data to be
trustworthy before they drive a decision.
NARRATION: Correlation is not causation, and apparent differences may be noise; do not over-claim from data.

SLIDE 11 | Visualisation and Dashboards
Choose charts that answer the question at a glance: lines for change over time, bars
to compare categories, scatter for relationships. Avoid misleading scales and
clutter. Dashboards put key metrics in front of decision-makers continuously, but
only work when they show the few numbers that drive action rather than everything
measurable.
NARRATION: Pick honest charts for the question, and build dashboards around the few metrics that drive action.

SLIDE 12 | Communicating Insight
A correct analysis no one understands changes nothing. Lead with the headline
finding, support it with one or two clear visuals, and end with a recommendation.
Tailor depth to the audience and state your assumptions and confidence. The analyst's
real product is not a chart but a decision made better, so communication is part of
the job, not an afterthought.
NARRATION: Lead with the finding, support with a clear visual, recommend an action, and state your confidence.

SLIDE 13 | Working Responsibly with Data
Handle data ethically and securely: protect personal and sensitive information,
follow data-protection rules and your organisation's policy, and watch for bias from
unrepresentative data. Make your work reproducible and your assumptions explicit so
others can trust and check it. Responsible practice protects both the people in the
data and the credibility of your analysis.
NARRATION: Protect personal data, follow policy, watch for bias, and keep your work reproducible and transparent.

SLIDE 14 | Course Summary and Next Steps
You now have the analyst's toolkit: frame a sharp question, pull data with SQL,
model and clean it, analyse with code and statistics, avoid the common traps,
visualise honestly, and communicate a clear recommendation. Take one real question
from your team, work it end to end, and present the result. Analytical skill grows by
doing it on data that matters.
NARRATION: Work one real question end to end, from SQL to recommendation, to put the analyst toolkit into practice.
