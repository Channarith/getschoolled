LESSON: Data & Insights for Business Decisions
LANGUAGE: en
AUDIENCE: corporate
TRACK: Data
LEVEL: Level 3 Apprenticeship
ROLE: Data Technician
DELIVERY: 13 month delivery
FIT: Anyone eager to boost data confidence.
SUMMARY: Enable your team to read, question, and act on data so everyday business decisions are grounded in evidence.

SLIDE 1 | What This Course Is About
This course builds practical data confidence for any role, with no technical
background required. You will learn to read everyday business data, ask the right
questions of it, avoid common traps, and turn numbers into decisions you can
defend. The goal is not to make you a data scientist. It is to make you a
clear-headed user of data who can challenge a chart, spot a misleading metric,
and explain what the evidence actually supports.
NARRATION: Welcome! This course builds everyday data confidence so you can turn numbers into sound decisions.

SLIDE 2 | Why Data Matters for Decisions
Most business choices are still made on gut feel, habit, or the loudest voice in
the room. Data does not remove judgment, but it grounds judgment in evidence so
teams argue about what to do rather than about what is true. A data-informed
culture is faster and fairer: it replaces opinion battles with shared facts, and
it lets you measure whether a decision actually worked instead of just assuming
it did.
NARRATION: Data grounds judgment in shared evidence, so teams debate what to do, not what is true.

SLIDE 3 | Data, Information, and Insight
Three words often get confused. Data is raw recorded facts, like a list of
sales transactions. Information is data organized to answer a question, like
total sales by region this month. Insight is the so-what: an understanding that
changes a decision, like noticing one region is shrinking because a competitor
opened nearby. The whole point of working with data is to climb from raw data up
to insight that drives action.
NARRATION: Data is raw facts, information is data organized to answer a question, insight changes a decision.

SLIDE 4 | Where Business Data Comes From
Data is created everywhere in a business: sales and point-of-sale systems,
customer records in a CRM, website and app analytics, support tickets, surveys,
finance and inventory systems, and spreadsheets people keep by hand. Each source
was built for its own purpose, so before trusting a number you should ask where
it came from, what it was originally for, and who is responsible for keeping it
accurate.
NARRATION: Business data comes from many systems; always ask where a number came from and who maintains it.

SLIDE 5 | Structured vs Unstructured Data
Structured data fits neatly into rows and columns, like a table of orders with
dates and amounts, and is easy to count and filter. Unstructured data has no
fixed shape, like emails, reviews, photos, or call recordings, and needs extra
work to analyze. Most businesses sit on far more unstructured data than they use.
Knowing which kind you have tells you how much effort the analysis will take.
NARRATION: Structured data fits rows and columns; unstructured data like text and images needs more work.

SLIDE 6 | Data Quality: Garbage In, Garbage Out
A beautiful chart built on bad data is still wrong, and often more dangerous
because it looks convincing. Watch for missing values, duplicates, inconsistent
formats, out-of-date records, and entries that are simply incorrect. Before
analyzing, sanity-check the data: do the totals look plausible, are the dates in
range, do categories match what you expect? Cleaning data is unglamorous but it
is where trustworthy analysis begins.
NARRATION: Bad data produces confident wrong answers; check quality before you trust any analysis.

SLIDE 7 | Asking a Good Question First
Analysis goes wrong when people pull numbers before deciding what they want to
know. Start with a clear, specific question tied to a decision: not "how are
sales?" but "which products lost the most revenue last quarter, and in which
regions?" A sharp question tells you exactly which data to gather and what a
useful answer looks like, and it stops you drowning in numbers that do not
matter.
NARRATION: Start with a specific question tied to a decision; it tells you which data actually matters.

SLIDE 8 | Descriptive Statistics: Summarizing Data
To make sense of many numbers you summarize them. The average, or mean, gives a
typical value, but it can be distorted by extremes. The median, the middle
value, is often more honest when a few huge or tiny values are present. The range
and spread tell you how much variation there is. A single summary number always
hides detail, so pair it with a sense of how spread out the data is.
NARRATION: Summaries like mean and median describe typical values, but always check the spread too.

SLIDE 9 | The Trap of Averages
Averages routinely mislead. The average customer may not exist: if half your
customers spend a little and half spend a lot, the average describes nobody. An
average wait time of five minutes can hide that some people wait an hour.
Whenever you see an average, ask what is being hidden underneath it, and look at
the distribution or break it into groups before drawing a conclusion.
NARRATION: An average can describe nobody; ask what variation it hides before trusting it.

SLIDE 10 | Correlation Is Not Causation
Two things moving together does not mean one causes the other. Ice cream sales
and sunburns rise together, but ice cream does not cause sunburn; hot weather
drives both. In business this matters enormously: a feature used by your best
customers may not be what makes them your best customers. Before acting on a
relationship, ask whether something else could explain it, and whether a
controlled test could confirm cause.
NARRATION: Things moving together is not proof one causes the other; look for a hidden common cause.

SLIDE 11 | Choosing the Right Chart
Charts make patterns visible, but the wrong chart hides them. Use a line chart
for change over time, a bar chart to compare categories, and a scatter plot to
show the relationship between two numbers. Pie charts work only for a few parts
of a whole and quickly become unreadable. The best chart answers the question at
a glance without the reader doing math in their head.
NARRATION: Match the chart to the question: lines for time, bars for comparison, scatter for relationships.

SLIDE 12 | How Charts Mislead
The same data can tell opposite stories depending on how it is drawn. A bar chart
whose axis does not start at zero exaggerates small differences. A cramped time
range can turn normal noise into a dramatic trend. Cherry-picked dates, dual
axes, and misleading scales are common, sometimes accidental, sometimes not.
Read the axes and the time range before you read the line, and be honest in the
charts you make.
NARRATION: Check the axes and time range first; truncated scales and cherry-picked ranges distort the story.

SLIDE 13 | Key Metrics and KPIs
A metric is any number you track; a key performance indicator, or KPI, is a
metric chosen because it reflects a goal that matters. Good KPIs are specific,
measurable, and tied to a decision someone can act on. Beware vanity metrics,
numbers that look impressive but change no decision, such as total page views
with no link to revenue or retention. Track the few numbers that would actually
change what you do.
NARRATION: KPIs are metrics tied to goals and decisions; avoid vanity metrics that change nothing.

SLIDE 14 | Segmentation: Breaking Data Into Groups
Totals hide stories. The real insight usually appears when you split data into
meaningful groups, by region, product, customer type, or time period. A flat
overall sales number might hide one segment booming and another collapsing.
Segmentation turns a vague "sales are okay" into a precise "new customers are
growing while repeat orders are falling," which points to a clear action.
NARRATION: Split totals into groups; segmentation reveals the stories that overall numbers hide.

SLIDE 15 | Telling the Story With Data
A correct analysis that no one understands changes nothing. Lead with the
headline finding, then support it with one or two clear visuals, and end with the
recommended action. Tailor depth to the audience: an executive wants the so-what
and the decision, an analyst wants the method. Always state your assumptions and
how confident you are, so people can judge how much weight to put on it.
NARRATION: Lead with the finding, support with a clear visual, end with the action, and state your confidence.

SLIDE 16 | Using Data Responsibly
Data about people carries responsibility. Protect personal and sensitive
information, follow your organization's data policy and relevant privacy rules,
and only use data for purposes people would reasonably expect. Be alert to bias:
if the data underrepresents a group, conclusions about that group will be weak or
unfair. Responsible data use protects both the people in the data and the trust
in your work.
NARRATION: Protect personal data, follow policy, and watch for bias from groups that are underrepresented.

SLIDE 17 | Course Summary and Next Steps
You can now climb from raw data to insight: start with a sharp question, check
data quality, summarize honestly, avoid the traps of averages and false
causation, choose truthful charts, track KPIs that matter, segment to find the
story, and communicate a clear recommendation. Practice on a real number from
your own work this week. Data confidence grows by doing.
NARRATION: Ask sharp questions, check quality, avoid traps, pick honest charts, and recommend a clear action.
