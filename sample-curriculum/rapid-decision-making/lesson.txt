LESSON: Rapid Decision-Making Under Pressure
LANGUAGE: en

SLIDE 1 | The Neuroscience of Split-Second Decisions
When a threat is detected, the amygdala triggers a stress response before
the prefrontal cortex (responsible for deliberate reasoning) is even aware
of the stimulus. This 200–500 ms gap is the window where trained reflex
either saves you or lets automatic fear responses take over.
Elite decision-makers in aviation, surgery, and special operations have
trained this window by thousands of repetitions — building "muscle memory"
that bypasses deliberation for the most critical actions.
NARRATION: You cannot think your way through a decision that must happen
in 400 milliseconds. But you can train the response that fires automatically.
That is exactly what rapid decision training does.

SLIDE 2 | Recognition-Primed Decision Making (RPD)
Gary Klein's RPD model describes how experts actually decide in the field.
They do not compare options — they recognize a pattern and simulate the
first plausible course of action mentally. If it "feels right," they act.
If the mental simulation fails, they cycle to the next option.
This is fundamentally different from classical decision theory (evaluate
all options, choose the best). RPD is faster, more robust under time pressure,
and explains why experts say "I just knew."
Training RPD: deliberate practice with many scenario variants builds the
pattern library. The expert's "intuition" is compressed experience.
NARRATION: Chess masters recognize 50,000+ patterns. Commercial pilots
have filed 10,000 hours of flight time. The apparent magic of expert
intuition is actually the result of systematic, deliberate practice.

SLIDE 3 | The Danger of Fast Decisions
Faster is not always better. Cognitive shortcuts introduce predictable errors:
- Plan continuation bias: continue the original plan even when conditions
  have changed (the leading cause of controlled flight into terrain)
- Sunk cost effect: commit further to a bad decision because of effort invested
- Fixation error: lock onto one explanation and filter out contradicting data
- Tunnel vision: narrowing of attention under stress to one object or task
Good rapid decision training explicitly trains WHEN to slow down — building
recognition of situations that require deliberate rather than intuitive processing.
NARRATION: The skill is not just deciding fast — it is knowing which decisions
can be fast and which ones demand a pause. That discernment is itself trainable.

SLIDE 4 | Pressure Calibration: The Yerkes-Dodson Curve
The Yerkes-Dodson law describes an inverted-U relationship between arousal
(stress/pressure) and performance. For complex tasks:
- Too little arousal: boredom, inattention, low motivation
- Optimal arousal: peak performance zone (the "flow state")
- Too much arousal: cognitive narrowing, working memory impairment, panic
Rapid decision training uses progressive pressure calibration to move your
optimal performance zone upward — increasing the arousal level at which
you still perform at peak. This is stress inoculation in action.
NARRATION: Top performers are not unaffected by pressure — they simply have
a higher ceiling. Training under gradually increasing pressure is the only
scientifically validated way to raise it.

SLIDE 5 | Aviation: Engine Failure Decisions
The engine-out decision tree is the canonical example of pre-programmed
rapid decision-making. Below 500 ft AGL on takeoff:
- Turn back to runway: PROHIBITED (insufficient altitude = stall/spin)
- Land straight ahead: REQUIRED (survivable forced landing in any clear area)
This rule is absolute. There is no deliberation, no "maybe I can make it."
The rule exists because accident data showed that attempting the turn-back
below 500 ft was fatal in nearly every case. The rule removes the choice.
NARRATION: The genius of the rule is that it removes a decision that a pilot
under extreme stress should not have to make in real time. The training
creates the reflex; the rule provides the authority to act on it.

SLIDE 6 | Medical: Triage in Mass Casualty Events
In mass casualty triage (START protocol), responders categorize patients
in under 60 seconds each:
- Black: expectant (not breathing after airway repositioning)
- Red: immediate (life-threatening, survivable with treatment)
- Yellow: delayed (serious but stable)
- Green: minor (walking wounded)
The most counterintuitive rule: the loudest, most demanding patient is
almost never the highest priority. The silent, pale patient with rapid
weak pulse (compensated shock) requires immediate intervention.
NARRATION: Triage violates every social instinct. You walk past the screaming
patient to reach the silent one. Only training and the cognitive framework of
START make this possible under the emotional weight of a mass casualty event.

SLIDE 7 | Cybersecurity: The Incident Response Window
In a ransomware incident, the first 30 minutes are decisive. The sequence:
1. Isolate (cut network segment — do not investigate first)
2. Preserve (memory snapshot before shutdown — forensic evidence)
3. Assess (backup integrity, blast radius)
4. Escalate (board, legal, CISO — in that order)
5. Communicate (regulator within legal window)
Every minute of delay before isolation allows lateral movement. An
organization that spends 20 minutes "investigating" before isolating
typically finds the infection has tripled in scope.
NARRATION: The incident response playbook is the cyber equivalent of the
emergency checklist. It removes deliberation from the highest-pressure phase
of the incident — exactly when cognitive load is highest.

SLIDE 8 | Training Method: Drills with Timed Windows
Effective rapid decision training uses:
- Short vignettes (2–3 sentences max) presenting the critical decision point
- Multiple plausible options (including tempting wrong answers)
- A time window calibrated to the domain (3–8 seconds for the most critical)
- Immediate feedback with rationale (not just "wrong — the answer is X")
- After-Decision Review identifying the key recognition cue
Drill sets begin at "deliberate" pace (no time pressure) to build the
correct mental model, then progressively tighten the window as accuracy improves.
NARRATION: The time window is the key variable. Too short too soon builds
speed without accuracy — producing dangerous overconfidence. The calibration
algorithm adapts to your performance, never pushing you past 85% accuracy.

SLIDE 9 | The After-Decision Review (ADR)
Every drill ends with an ADR that answers:
1. What was the key recognition cue in this situation?
2. What decision did you make, and why?
3. If incorrect: what made the wrong option tempting?
4. What is the expert's decision, and what is the rationale?
5. What would you look for next time to make this faster?
The ADR is as important as the drill itself. Speed without accurate
pattern recognition is dangerous. The ADR builds the mental model
that makes the next drill faster and more accurate.
NARRATION: Drill without debrief is practice of an uncertain performance.
Drill with ADR is deliberate practice — the only kind that produces
genuine improvement.

SLIDE 10 | Building Your Rapid Decision Skill Path
Your personalised skill path:
1. Domain assessment (which domain has highest stakes for you?)
2. Deliberate pace drills (build pattern recognition, no time pressure)
3. Moderate pressure (2× expert time — still deliberate but feels rushed)
4. Time-critical (1.1× expert time — no margin for deliberation)
5. Split-second (0.6× expert time — pure reflex from trained pattern)
6. Cross-domain transfer (use aviation patterns in medical context and vice versa)
The AI agent tracks your accuracy and time-to-decision across all drills,
calibrates the pressure level to your performance, and flags the
behavioral patterns that need attention.
NARRATION: This is not about becoming a robot. It is about building a
cognitive toolkit that is accessible even when everything around you
is telling you to panic. You train this — and it is always there when you need it.
