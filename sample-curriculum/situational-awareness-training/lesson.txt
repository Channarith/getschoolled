LESSON: Situational Awareness Training — OODA and DECIDE in Practice
LANGUAGE: en

SLIDE 1 | What Is Situational Awareness?
Situational awareness (SA) is the accurate perception of the elements in
the environment within a volume of time and space, the comprehension of
their meaning, and the projection of their status in the near future.
It is NOT the same as paying attention — it is structured, active mental
modelling of a changing environment. Airline captains, ER physicians, air
traffic controllers, and military commanders all train SA as a primary skill.
NARRATION: Situational awareness is the foundation on which every good
decision in a dynamic environment rests. Without it, the best procedures
in the world cannot save you.

SLIDE 2 | Level 1 SA — Perceiving the Environment
Level 1 (Perception) is about raw data gathering. It requires:
- A structured scan pattern (not random glancing)
- Knowing what to look for (instrument scan, threat cues, patient vitals)
- Monitoring channels that are easy to neglect (temperature, background noise)
- Recognising absence as a cue (the dog that didn't bark — no alarm sounding)
Common failures: channelled attention (fixating on one instrument), cognitive
tunnelling (high workload narrows the scan to a single track), and inattentional
blindness (missing unexpected stimuli when task-focused).
NARRATION: The first enemy of Level 1 SA is busyness. The higher your
workload, the narrower your scan becomes — exactly when you need it widest.
SLIDE 3 | Level 2 SA — Comprehending the Picture
Level 2 (Comprehension) requires integrating perceived cues into a coherent
mental model. This is where pattern recognition from experience does the
heavy lifting. A cardiologist looking at an ECG trace is doing Level 2 SA —
they see the same squiggles as a layperson but their mental model transforms
raw data into "inferior ST elevation — probable STEMI."
Training Level 2 SA means:
- Deliberate exposure to many scenario variants
- After-action analysis of what cues were missed and why
- Explicit mental model building ("if X then probably Y")
NARRATION: Level 2 is where training pays its dividends. Every scenario you
practice adds another pattern to your recognition library.

SLIDE 4 | Level 3 SA — Projecting the Future
Level 3 (Projection) means predicting how the situation will evolve over
the next 30 seconds, 5 minutes, or 2 hours. It enables proactive rather
than reactive action. Examples:
- Pilot: "With this headwind, I will be 20 nm short of fuel on current routing"
- Doctor: "This patient's pressure trend means shock within 10 minutes"
- Incident responder: "Ransomware has been active 6 hours; backup is probably intact"
Level 3 failures often manifest as "tunnel vision" on the current state,
missing the trajectory. The fix: build prediction into the monitoring cycle.
NARRATION: Level 3 SA transforms a reactive firefighter into a proactive
chess player. It is the most trainable level and the highest-leverage skill.

SLIDE 5 | OODA Loop — Cycle Time Is Everything
Boyd's insight was that the OODA loop is not a one-time process — it
cycles continuously, and the agent with the shorter loop controls the
encounter. Key implications:
- Disrupting the adversary's OODA loop (introducing fog, speed, novelty)
  is as powerful as improving your own
- Re-orientation (updating your mental model when new data arrives) is the
  hardest and most critical phase
- Excessive deliberation at Decide slows the loop fatally
- Pre-built decision rules (if-then scripts from training) short-circuit
  the Decide phase and massively accelerate action
NARRATION: Every emergency checklist is a pre-built if-then script designed
to collapse the DECIDE phase. We build them in training so we don't have to
construct them under fire.

SLIDE 6 | The DECIDE Model in Aviation Context
The Federal Aviation Administration teaches DECIDE to student pilots
because novice pilots under stress default to the first option they
think of — which is often wrong. The DECIDE model enforces:
D — Detect the problem (name it explicitly)
E — Estimate the need for action (what happens if I do nothing?)
C — Choose the desired outcome (what do I want to achieve?)
I — Identify actions needed (what resources do I have?)
D — Do the necessary actions (commit and execute)
E — Evaluate the effect (is it working? what is my next decision point?)
NARRATION: Notice the model ends with Evaluate, not Do. That is intentional.
In a dynamic situation, action is not the end of the process — it is the
beginning of the next OBSERVE phase.

SLIDE 7 | Scenario Practice: Engine Roughness at Cruise
Scenario: You are at 8,500 ft, OAT -5°C. The engine develops a roughness.
EGT rises 50°F. Fuel flow is normal.
OODA Application:
- Observe: engine roughness (audible), EGT +50°F (visual), OAT (visual), fuel flow (visual)
- Orient: OAT in icing range + roughness + rising EGT = probable carb ice
- Decide: apply carb heat; monitor 60 s; if no improvement, declare precautionary
- Act: carb heat ON; begin diversion assessment
Critical cue: OAT -5°C places the engine squarely in the carburetor ice
risk band (-15°C to +5°C with high humidity). Miss this cue and you may
diagnose a fuel problem instead.
NARRATION: Notice that the correct decision follows directly from correct
orientation. Misidentifying the problem leads to wrong action — enriching
mixture instead of applying carb heat, making the situation worse.

SLIDE 8 | Scenario Practice: Medical Emergency in Public
Scenario: Adult found collapsed in shopping mall. Unresponsive, agonal
breathing. AED 30 m away. Two bystanders present.
DECIDE Application:
D — Detect: cardiac arrest (unresponsive + absent normal breathing)
E — Estimate: without CPR, survival drops ~10% per minute
C — Choose: start CPR + retrieve AED + call emergency services simultaneously
I — Identify: two bystanders (send one for AED; one calls), own hands for CPR
D — Do: begin chest compressions; direct bystanders
E — Evaluate: depth ≥ 5 cm, rate 100–120/min; AED arriving; EMS en route
Common mistake: running to get the AED before starting CPR. The 30-second
delay is costlier than a 30-second AED delay.
NARRATION: The DECIDE model prevents the instinct to "go get help" before
the most critical intervention. In cardiac arrest, the CPR-giver IS the help.

SLIDE 9 | Building Your Situational Scan
A repeatable SA scan process for any high-stakes environment:
1. Define your key indicators (what 5–7 things will tell you the situation
   is changing in a dangerous way?)
2. Set your scan frequency (how often must each be checked?)
3. Build alerting thresholds (what value triggers action?)
4. Share the picture (brief your team on what you are monitoring and why)
5. Pre-brief threat cues (what are the three most likely things to go wrong
   in this mission/procedure/shift?)
NARRATION: Professional pilots brief threat cues before every flight.
They walk through the "what if" before takeoff so the answer is
pre-computed, not invented under pressure.

SLIDE 10 | Debrief: The Learning Loop
No SA training cycle is complete without debrief. The structured AAR format:
1. What did we intend to happen?
2. What actually happened?
3. Why was there a difference?
4. What will we change next time?
The most important rule of debrief: psychological safety. A team that
cannot honestly name its errors learns nothing. The best-performing teams
in military, aviation, and medicine have cultures where debrief is routine,
expected, and blame-free.
NARRATION: The debrief transforms a single experience into a shared lesson.
Multiplied over thousands of scenarios, it is the mechanism by which
individual performance compounds into team excellence.
