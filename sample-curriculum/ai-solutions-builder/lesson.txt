TRACK: AI
LEVEL: Level 4 Apprenticeship
ROLE: AI & Automation Practitioner

FIT: Mid-level employees who serve as departmental agentic AI experts and champions.
SUMMARY: Create agentic AI experts who build solutions to improve team processes and eliminate repetitive tasks.

SLIDE 1 | What This Course Is About
This course turns capable professionals into builders of practical AI solutions
who can identify repetitive work in their team and automate it with agentic AI.
You will learn to spot good automation candidates, design AI agents and workflows,
connect AI to real tools and data, and ship solutions that are reliable, safe, and
measurable. You should already be comfortable using GenAI tools; here you move
from user to builder.
NARRATION: This course moves you from using AI to building agentic solutions that automate real team work.

SLIDE 2 | From Assistant to Agent
A chat assistant answers a single prompt. An AI agent pursues a goal over multiple
steps: it plans, calls tools, observes results, and decides what to do next until
the task is done. For example, an agent might read a support ticket, look up the
customer, draft a reply, and file a follow-up task. The shift from one-shot
answers to goal-directed action is what makes agents powerful and what makes them
need careful design.
NARRATION: An agent pursues a goal over multiple steps by planning, calling tools, and acting, not just answering once.

SLIDE 3 | Finding the Right Problems to Automate
Good automation candidates are repetitive, rule-ish, high-volume, and low-risk to
get wrong on the first pass. Map your team's recurring tasks and rank them by time
spent, frequency, and how tolerant they are of occasional error. Start with a
painful but contained task rather than the most complex one. A narrow, well-chosen
first project builds skill and trust faster than an ambitious one that stalls.
NARRATION: Pick repetitive, high-volume, low-risk tasks first; a narrow win builds skill and trust fastest.

SLIDE 4 | Anatomy of an Agentic Workflow
Most agentic solutions share a shape: a trigger starts it, the agent reasons about
the goal, it calls tools to fetch data or take actions, it checks results, and it
produces an output or hands off to a human. Designing a solution means defining
each of these clearly: what starts it, what it is allowed to do, what good output
looks like, and when it should stop and ask a person.
NARRATION: Agentic workflows have a trigger, reasoning, tool calls, checks, and a human handoff; design each clearly.

SLIDE 5 | Tools, Functions, and Integrations
Agents become useful when they can act on real systems through tools, also called
functions: searching a knowledge base, querying a database, sending an email,
creating a ticket, or calling an API. Each tool needs a clear description, defined
inputs and outputs, and limits on what it can do. The set of tools you give an
agent defines both its capability and its blast radius.
NARRATION: Tools let agents act on real systems; each needs clear inputs, outputs, and limits on what it can do.

SLIDE 6 | Grounding Agents with Retrieval
Agents that answer questions about your business need access to your knowledge.
Retrieval-augmented generation fetches relevant documents and feeds them to the
model so answers are grounded in your real, current content instead of the model's
general training. Good retrieval, well-organised sources, sensible chunking, and
relevance ranking, is often the difference between a useful agent and a confidently
wrong one.
NARRATION: Retrieval feeds agents your real documents so answers are grounded, not guessed.

SLIDE 7 | Prompt and Instruction Design for Agents
An agent's system instructions are its job description: its role, its goals, the
tools it may use, its constraints, and how it should behave when uncertain. Be
explicit about what it must never do and when to defer to a human. Clear, bounded
instructions make agents predictable; vague ones make them wander. Treat the
instruction as a living spec you refine as you observe behaviour.
NARRATION: An agent's instructions define its role, tools, limits, and when to defer to a human; keep them explicit.

SLIDE 8 | Keeping Humans in the Loop
Reliable solutions decide deliberately where a human approves or reviews. High-
stakes or irreversible actions, sending money, emailing customers, changing
records, should pause for human approval, at least until the agent has earned
trust. Design clear checkpoints and make it easy for a person to see what the
agent did and why. Autonomy is something you grant gradually, based on evidence.
NARRATION: Put human approval on high-stakes actions and grant autonomy gradually as the agent earns trust.

SLIDE 9 | Testing and Evaluating AI Solutions
Because AI output varies, you cannot test it like ordinary code with one expected
answer. Build a set of realistic example cases with good outcomes, run the agent
against them, and score quality, accuracy, and safety. Re-run this evaluation
whenever you change the prompt, model, or tools. Measurement turns "it seemed to
work" into evidence you can trust and improve.
NARRATION: Evaluate agents against realistic example cases and re-test on every change; measurement builds trust.

SLIDE 10 | Reliability, Cost, and Monitoring
In production, watch for failures, runaway loops, latency, and cost, since every
model call and tool use has a price. Set limits on steps and spend, add retries
and fallbacks for tool errors, and log what the agent does so you can debug and
audit it. A solution is only as valuable as it is reliable and affordable to run.
NARRATION: Monitor failures, latency, and cost; cap steps and spend, and log agent actions for debugging and audit.

SLIDE 11 | Responsible and Secure Automation
Automating work raises real risks: agents can leak data through tools, take wrong
actions at scale, or embed bias. Limit each tool's permissions to the minimum
needed, protect sensitive data, guard against prompt injection from untrusted
content, and keep humans accountable for outcomes. Build governance and security
in from the start, agreed with stakeholders, not bolted on later.
NARRATION: Limit tool permissions, protect data, guard against prompt injection, and keep humans accountable.

SLIDE 12 | Driving Adoption and Measuring Impact
A solution only delivers value if people use it and it actually saves time or
improves quality. Involve the team early, make the agent easy to use, and measure
the before-and-after: hours saved, errors reduced, turnaround time. Champions like
you make adoption real by supporting colleagues and feeding their problems back
into the next iteration.
NARRATION: Drive adoption by involving the team and measuring real before-and-after impact like hours saved.

SLIDE 13 | Course Summary and Next Steps
You can now spot automatable work, design an agentic workflow, ground it with
retrieval, give it safe tools, keep humans in the loop, evaluate it, and ship it
responsibly. Choose one repetitive task in your team, build the simplest agent
that helps, measure the impact, and iterate. That is how an AI champion turns
ideas into working solutions.
NARRATION: Build the simplest useful agent for one repetitive task, measure impact, and iterate.
