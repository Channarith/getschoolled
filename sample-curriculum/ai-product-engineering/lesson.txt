LESSON: AI Product Engineering
LANGUAGE: en
AUDIENCE: corporate
TRACK: Engineering
LEVEL: Level 6 Degree Apprenticeship
ROLE: AI Product Engineer
DELIVERY: 21 months delivery, plus 3 months assessment
FIT: Engineers ready to innovate and integrate AI to elevate product development.
SUMMARY: Transform software engineers into AI-enabled product specialists who design, build, and ship AI-powered features at production quality.

SLIDE 1 | What This Course Is About
This course turns experienced software engineers into AI product engineers who can
design and ship AI-powered features users rely on. You will learn to use foundation
models and generative AI as building blocks, design and evaluate AI features, deploy
them reliably, and do it responsibly. It assumes solid software engineering and adds the
distinct skills of building products on top of probabilistic AI systems.
NARRATION: This course turns software engineers into AI product engineers who ship reliable AI-powered features.

SLIDE 2 | How AI Products Are Different
Building with AI breaks some software assumptions. Traditional code is deterministic, the
same input gives the same output, while AI models are probabilistic and can give different,
sometimes wrong, answers. So you design for uncertainty: handle variability, plan for
errors, and measure quality statistically rather than expecting one correct result. This
mindset shift underpins everything else.
NARRATION: AI products are probabilistic, not deterministic, so design for uncertainty and measure quality statistically.

SLIDE 3 | Foundation Models as Building Blocks
Modern AI features are usually built on foundation models, including large language
models, accessed through APIs rather than trained from scratch. You compose products by
prompting, adapting, and orchestrating these models. Understanding their capabilities,
limits, context windows, and costs lets you choose the right model for a feature and use
it effectively as a component.
NARRATION: AI features are built on foundation models accessed via APIs; know their capabilities, limits, and costs.

SLIDE 4 | Prompt Engineering and Structured Output
The interface to a model is the prompt. Engineering reliable prompts means specifying
role, task, context, and format, and often requiring structured output like JSON so your
code can use the result. Constrain, give examples, and handle the model's variability.
Treat prompts as part of the codebase, versioned and tested, not as throwaway strings.
NARRATION: Engineer prompts with clear role, task, context, and structured output, and version and test them like code.

SLIDE 5 | Retrieval-Augmented Generation
To make AI features accurate about your domain, ground them in your data with retrieval:
fetch relevant documents and supply them to the model so answers reflect your real,
current content. Good RAG, sensible chunking, quality embeddings, and relevance ranking,
is often the difference between a reliable feature and a confidently wrong one. It also
keeps knowledge current without retraining.
NARRATION: Retrieval-augmented generation grounds AI features in your own data for accurate, current answers.

SLIDE 6 | Building Agents and Tool Use
Beyond single answers, you can build agents that pursue goals over multiple steps,
calling tools and APIs to fetch data and take actions. This unlocks powerful features but
adds complexity: you must bound what the agent can do, handle multi-step failures, and
decide where humans approve actions. Design agent capability and guardrails together.
NARRATION: Agents pursue goals using tools over multiple steps; design their capability and guardrails together.

SLIDE 7 | Fine-Tuning and Adaptation
Sometimes prompting and retrieval are not enough and you adapt a model, for example
fine-tuning on examples to specialise behaviour or format. Adaptation can improve quality
and consistency but adds cost and maintenance. Know the options, prompting, retrieval,
fine-tuning, and choose the lightest one that meets the need, since heavier approaches are
harder to change as models evolve.
NARRATION: Adapt models via fine-tuning when prompting and retrieval fall short, choosing the lightest approach that works.

SLIDE 8 | Evaluating AI Features
You cannot test AI like deterministic code. Build evaluation sets of realistic inputs with
good outcomes, and score quality, accuracy, safety, and relevance, using automated metrics
and human review. Re-run evaluation on every change to prompts, models, or data.
Systematic evaluation is what turns "it seems to work" into evidence and prevents silent
regressions.
NARRATION: Evaluate AI features against realistic test sets scoring quality and safety, and re-test on every change.

SLIDE 9 | Productionising and LLMOps
Shipping AI features needs operational discipline: manage prompts, models, and versions;
control latency and the real cost of model calls; add caching, retries, and fallbacks; and
monitor quality in production. Models and APIs change, so design to swap them. This LLMOps
practice is what keeps AI features reliable and affordable once real users depend on them.
NARRATION: Productionise AI with version control, cost and latency management, fallbacks, and quality monitoring.

SLIDE 10 | UX for AI: Designing for Imperfection
Great AI products are designed around the fact that AI is sometimes wrong. Show confidence
and sources, make it easy to verify and correct output, keep humans in control of
important actions, and set honest expectations. Good AI UX builds trust by being
transparent about uncertainty rather than hiding it, turning a fallible model into a
helpful experience.
NARRATION: Design AI UX for imperfection: show sources, enable correction, and keep humans in control.

SLIDE 11 | Responsible AI and Safety
AI features carry real risks: hallucinations, bias, privacy leaks, prompt injection from
untrusted input, and misuse. Build safeguards, validate and constrain inputs and outputs,
protect data, test for bias, and keep human oversight on high-stakes decisions. Bake safety
and governance into the engineering from the start; for AI products it is a core
requirement, not an add-on.
NARRATION: Build in safeguards against hallucination, bias, privacy leaks, and prompt injection, with human oversight.

SLIDE 12 | Product Thinking and Measuring Value
Engineering excellence is wasted if the feature does not help users. Start from a real user
problem, ship a small version, and measure whether it delivers value, adoption, task
success, satisfaction, cost. AI is a means, not the goal. The best AI product engineers
pair technical depth with judgment about what is worth building and how to prove it works.
NARRATION: Start from a real user problem, ship small, and measure value; AI is a means, not the goal.

SLIDE 13 | Course Summary and Next Steps
You can now build AI features on foundation models: engineer prompts and structured output,
ground with retrieval, add agents and adaptation where justified, evaluate rigorously,
productionise with LLMOps, design for imperfection, and ship responsibly. Take one real user
problem, build the simplest AI feature that helps, evaluate it, and put it in production
behind good guardrails.
NARRATION: Build the simplest useful AI feature for a real problem, evaluate it, and ship it with guardrails.
