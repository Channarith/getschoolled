LESSON: Intro to Photosynthesis
LANGUAGE: en

SLIDE 1 | What is Photosynthesis?
Photosynthesis is the process by which green plants, algae, and some bacteria
convert light energy into chemical energy stored in sugars. It is the foundation
of almost every food chain on Earth.
NARRATION: Welcome! Today we learn how plants make their own food using sunlight.

SLIDE 2 | The Inputs
Photosynthesis needs three inputs: carbon dioxide from the air, water from the
roots, and light energy from the sun. Chlorophyll, the green pigment, captures
the light.
NARRATION: Plants take in carbon dioxide and water, and capture sunlight with chlorophyll.

SLIDE 3 | The Outputs
The outputs of photosynthesis are glucose, a sugar the plant uses for energy,
and oxygen, which is released into the air for us to breathe.
NARRATION: The plant produces glucose for energy and releases oxygen we breathe.

SLIDE 4 | The Equation
The balanced equation is: six carbon dioxide plus six water, using light energy,
yields one glucose plus six oxygen. This happens mainly in the leaves.
NARRATION: Six CO2 plus six water, with light, makes glucose and six oxygen.

SLIDE 5 | Where It Happens: The Chloroplast
Photosynthesis takes place in organelles called chloroplasts, found mostly in
the cells of leaves. Inside the chloroplast, stacks of disc-shaped thylakoids
hold the chlorophyll, while the fluid around them is called the stroma.
NARRATION: It happens inside chloroplasts, in the thylakoids and the surrounding stroma.

SLIDE 6 | The Light-Dependent Reactions
In the thylakoid membranes, chlorophyll absorbs sunlight and splits water
molecules. This releases oxygen and stores energy in two carrier molecules, ATP
and NADPH.
NARRATION: Light splits water in the thylakoids, releasing oxygen and making ATP and NADPH.

SLIDE 7 | The Calvin Cycle
The light-independent reactions, called the Calvin cycle, happen in the stroma.
They use the ATP and NADPH to fix carbon dioxide into glucose. No light is used
directly, but the cycle depends on the products of the light reactions.
NARRATION: The Calvin cycle uses ATP and NADPH to turn carbon dioxide into sugar.

SLIDE 8 | Chlorophyll and Light
Chlorophyll absorbs light most strongly in the blue and red parts of the
spectrum and reflects green, which is why leaves look green. Some plants have
extra pigments that capture other colors of light.
NARRATION: Chlorophyll absorbs blue and red light and reflects green, so leaves look green.

SLIDE 9 | Leaf Structure and Gas Exchange
Leaves are adapted for photosynthesis: they are broad and flat to catch light,
and tiny pores called stomata let carbon dioxide in and oxygen out. Guard cells
open and close the stomata to control water loss.
NARRATION: Stomata are tiny pores that let gases in and out, controlled by guard cells.

SLIDE 10 | Limiting Factors
The rate of photosynthesis is limited by light intensity, carbon dioxide
concentration, and temperature. If any one is too low, it slows the whole
process, even when the others are plentiful.
NARRATION: Light, carbon dioxide, and temperature each can limit how fast photosynthesis goes.

SLIDE 11 | Why Photosynthesis Matters
Photosynthesis provides the food energy for nearly all living things and the
oxygen most organisms need to breathe. It also removes carbon dioxide from the
atmosphere, helping regulate Earth's climate.
NARRATION: Photosynthesis feeds the planet, supplies oxygen, and removes carbon dioxide.

SLIDE 12 | Photosynthesis and Respiration
Cellular respiration is almost the reverse of photosynthesis: organisms break
glucose back down with oxygen to release energy, producing carbon dioxide and
water. Together the two processes cycle carbon and energy through ecosystems.
NARRATION: Respiration reverses photosynthesis, cycling carbon and energy through life.

FACT: Chlorophyll is the green pigment that absorbs light for photosynthesis.
FACT: Photosynthesis occurs in the chloroplasts of plant cells.
FACT: Oxygen is a byproduct of photosynthesis and is essential for animal life.
FACT: The light-dependent reactions produce ATP and NADPH and release oxygen from water.
FACT: The Calvin cycle fixes carbon dioxide into glucose using ATP and NADPH.
FACT: Stomata are pores in the leaf that allow carbon dioxide in and oxygen out.
FACT: Light intensity, carbon dioxide, and temperature are the main limiting factors of photosynthesis.
FACT: Cellular respiration releases energy from glucose and is roughly the reverse of photosynthesis.
